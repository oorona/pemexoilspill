# **Maritime Forensic Analysis of Assets Associated with Petróleos Mexicanos (Pemex) and Third-Party Contractors in the Context of the February 2026 Gulf of Mexico Hydrocarbon Release**

The hydrocarbon discharge identified in the Gulf of Mexico during the first quarter of 2026 represents one of the most significant environmental and operational challenges for the Mexican energy sector in recent years. Initial reports of an oil slick emerged around February 1, 2026, situated primarily in the prolific waters of the Sonda de Campeche, an area dominated by the infrastructure of the state-owned oil company, Petróleos Mexicanos (Pemex).1 As the slick expanded to cover an estimated 50 square kilometers by mid-February—an area described by environmental observers as approximately 1,000 times the size of Mexico City’s Zócalo—the necessity for precise maritime identification became paramount.2 This report provides a comprehensive directory of maritime assets associated with Pemex, including proprietary vessels and those operated by strategic service contractors, to facilitate the filtration of regional Automatic Identification System (AIS) data and isolate units of interest during the critical window of early February 2026\.

## **Chronological and Environmental Context of the 2026 Incident**

The detection of oil washing ashore on the coasts of Veracruz and Tabasco prompted immediate inquiries into the integrity of the subsea pipeline network and the operational status of offshore platforms.1 By late February and early March 2017, localized government reports began to merge into a broader regional crisis, as tar patches and crude oil residues affected seven nature reserves and decimated the local fishing industry during the high-demand Lenten season.1  
The discrepancies between official government narratives and independent satellite monitoring have fueled a transparency crisis. Federal authorities, including the Mexican Navy (SEMAR) and the Department of the Environment and Natural Resources, initially suggested that the spill might have originated from natural seeps or an "unidentified vessel" not associated with the state oil company.1 However, interdisciplinary analysis by groups such as Greenpeace and the Mexican Center for Environmental Law (Cemda) utilized Copernicus satellite imagery to pinpoint the origin near the Abkatún platform and a major pipeline transporting Maya crude from the Cantarell field to the Dos Bocas marine terminal.3  
The slick was observed to be active at least until February 17, 2026, dispersing gradually due to ocean currents and weather conditions, which eventually carried the pollutants over 600 kilometers of coastline.2 This sustained release, rather than a one-time discharge, strongly implies a structural failure in the subsea infrastructure rather than a simple illegal dump by a transiting merchant vessel.2

### **Impact on Coastal Economies and Ecosystems**

The socio-economic repercussions of the spill have been particularly severe in Veracruz, where the local fishing industry provides the primary livelihood for thousands of families.5 The presence of oil in the water led to a dramatic drop in fish supplies and a collapse in consumer demand, as markets in seaside cities remained empty during Holy Week.5 Beyond the economic damage, the environmental toll includes reported deaths of sea turtles, fish, and eels, as well as contamination of the Veracruz Coral Reef System.3

## **Identifying the Pemex Maritime Signature**

To effectively filter the vast quantity of AIS data from the Gulf of Mexico, it is necessary to understand the naming conventions and identification codes used by Mexican-flagged and Pemex-associated units. The Maritime Mobile Service Identity (MMSI) for Mexican vessels follows the format 345XXXXXX, where "345" is the Maritime Identification Digit (MID) allocated to Mexico by the International Telecommunication Union (ITU).7

### **Proprietary Fleet: Pemex Logística and PMI Norteamérica**

Pemex manages its core maritime logistics through Pemex Logística and its international trading arm, PMI. This fleet is responsible for the distribution of refined products (gasoline, diesel) and the handling of crude oil exports.10 As of early 2026, Pemex Logística operated a fleet of 16 refined product tankers, with 14 owned directly and two under financial lease.10  
The proprietary fleet is divided strategically between the Pacific and Gulf coasts. In the Gulf of Mexico, which comprises 46.67% of the fleet's deployment, these vessels primarily transport distillates (71.43%) and heavy fuels (28.57%).10

## **Strategic Service Contractors and Specialized Offshore Units**

Because Pemex relies heavily on third-party companies for technical subsea work, well stimulation, and personnel transport, the vessels belonging to these contractors are frequently the most relevant when investigating infrastructure failures. These companies operate under specialized charters and their vessels often possess the equipment necessary to address pipeline leaks or platform fires.

### **The Role of the Árbol Grande in the February Incident**

The case of the *Árbol Grande* (IMO 9264867, MMSI 345070403\) is central to the allegations of an official cover-up regarding the 2026 spill.4 Satellite data identified this vessel stationary for approximately 200 hours between February 9 and February 16, 2026, directly over an active pipeline.6 This pipeline connects the offshore platforms of the Cantarell field to the shore facilities at Dos Bocas.6  
The *Árbol Grande* is a Diving Support Vessel (DSV) specialized in subsea pipeline maintenance and subsea intervention.6 While Pemex officials asserted the vessel was merely conducting "preventive inspections," its prolonged stationary presence at a site coincident with a major oil slick has led independent analysts to conclude it was engaged in emergency repairs to a rupture.4

## **Floating Production, Storage, and Offloading (FPSO) Assets**

The maritime activity of the Gulf of Mexico is anchored by large-scale FPSO units, which serve as permanent hubs for crude oil production and offloading. These vessels do not move, but they are the destination for nearly all tankers and supply vessels in the region.29

### **FPSO Yúum K'ak' Náab (El Señor del Mar)**

The *Yúum K'ak' Náab* (IMO 7708302\) is the most critical asset in the Ku-Maloob-Zaap field complex, which produces approximately 40% of Mexico's total oil output.29 In July 2022, Pemex assumed full ownership and operation of this FPSO from BW Offshore after the conclusion of a 15-year financial lease.31  
The history of this vessel includes multiple environmental and safety incidents. In July 2021, a subsea pipeline burst nearby, creating the viral "Eye of Fire" gas leak.29 More recently, in August 2023, the FPSO had to shut down temporarily due to a leak from one of its offloading hose trains.29 Because the KMZ complex pipes its oil to the *Yúum K'ak' Náab* for export, any failure in the subsea network near this vessel would result in a massive and sustained hydrocarbon release.29

## **Forensic Filtering Strategy for the February 2026 Slick**

Given the data provided, a researcher can apply the following filtering criteria to their list of MMSI and geolocation points to isolate the most likely suspects or response units related to the February 1 oil slick:

1. **Flag and MID Filter**: Isolate all vessels with an MMSI starting with 345 (Mexico).7  
2. **Stationary Analysis**: Identify vessels that remained stationary (speed \< 0.5 knots) for more than 48 hours in the vicinity of the Abkatún, Cantarell, or Ku-Maloob-Zaap fields during the first two weeks of February. The *Árbol Grande* case proves that stationary behavior is a key indicator of subsea repair work.4  
3. **Proximity to Known Pipelines**: Focus on vessels located directly over the pipeline corridor running from Cantarell to Dos Bocas. This was the site of the suspected leak identified by satellite imagery.6  
4. **Specialized Type Filter**: Prioritize Diving Support Vessels (DSV), Well Stimulation Vessels, and Multi-Purpose Support Vessels (MPSV). These units possess the technical capability to address a high-pressure subsea leak.17  
5. **Shuttle Tanker Patterns**: Analyze the arrival and departure patterns at the *Yúum K'ak' Náab* FPSO and the Cayo Arcas terminal. Any sudden cessation of offloading activity on or around February 1 would corroborate a leak that forced a system shutdown.12

## **The Policy and Regulatory Environment Under the Sheinbaum Administration**

The management of the 2026 spill has highlights the ongoing tension between Mexico's state-centric energy policy and the growing demand for environmental accountability. President Claudia Sheinbaum, who assumed office following a campaign that balanced energy sovereignty with a commitment to scientific management, has faced criticism for the interdisciplinary group’s lack of transparency.2  
While the government has formed a special task force involving the Navy and energy regulators, the delay in acknowledging the scale of the spill and the initial attempts to blame "natural seeps" have created a trust gap with international observers and local communities.1 For maritime researchers, this means that official government data may be incomplete or biased, making independent AIS and satellite analysis the most reliable method for determining the true cause and responsible parties.3

## **Conclusion**

The hydrocarbon release that began on February 1, 2026, was not a localized incident but a massive regional event that exposed the vulnerabilities of aging offshore infrastructure and the complexities of Pemex's maritime logistics network. By utilizing the names, IMO numbers, and MMSI identifiers provided in this report, investigators can filter the overwhelming volume of AIS data to focus on the units that truly matter: the proprietary tankers of Pemex Logística, the specialized repair vessels like the *Árbol Grande*, and the ubiquitous support fleet of contractors like Cotemar and Naviera Integral.  
The 50-square-kilometer slick that eventually coated the shores of Veracruz and Tabasco is more than an environmental tragedy; it is a data-driven forensic puzzle.2 The presence of specific vessels at specific times over active pipeline corridors provides the "fingerprint" of the incident. This directory serves as the essential key to decyphering those fingerprints and ensuring that the operational reality of the Gulf of Mexico's oil fields is brought into the light of public and scientific scrutiny.
